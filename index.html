Why AGRICAF?

Good Forecasts Should Come With Good Explanations

In February 2022, a tonne of wheat cost around $340 on the global market. Four months later, it cost nearly $440 — a 30% increase caused by Russia's invasion of Ukraine. In France or Germany, bread prices rose by a few percent. In Egypt, which sources over 60% of its wheat from Russia and Ukraine, the shock threatened the livelihoods of millions. Across East Africa, it compounded an ongoing drought to push communities toward famine.

When global commodity prices spike, high-income countries absorb the shock. Low-income countries bear the full force.

What makes these crises especially damaging is not just the price level — it's the surprise. Farmers in developing regions often have no reliable way to anticipate whether prices will rise or fall in coming months. Without that information, they cannot decide when to sell their harvest, how much to store, or what to plant next season. The information that would allow better decisions exists — buried in trade data, stock reports, and price histories — but it is not packaged in a way that reaches the people who need it most.

What Already Exists — and What Is Missing

Existing monitoring platforms like FAO's FPMA and WFP's Economic Explorer do excellent work tracking food prices. But they monitor without forecasting — they tell you what prices did last month, not what they are likely to do next month. For a policymaker or farmer planning ahead, this is like driving by looking only in the rearview mirror.

Forecasting tools do exist, but they are locked behind expensive subscriptions designed for corporate clients, rely on proprietary data unavailable to the public, and typically operate as black boxes — users receive a prediction but no insight into how it was generated or what assumptions drove the result.

What AGRICAF Does Differently

It forecasts. AGRICAF produces monthly price forecasts up to twelve months ahead for five global agricultural commodities: wheat, maize, soybean, arabica coffee, and sugar. All inputs come from publicly available sources.

It explains every forecast. For each prediction, AGRICAF reports which input features contributed most, how much each contributed, and whether each feature pushed the price up or down. Explanations are organised into four communication levels — from a global annual overview down to a specific forecast for a specific month and year — so that different users can engage at different depths.

It is fully open. Every step in the pipeline is documented and reproducible. A cooperative, a government ministry, or a research institute can run it independently, update forecasts when new data arrives, and audit every calculation.

Who It Is For

AGRICAF is designed for researchers in agricultural economics and food policy, analysts at food security organisations who need to understand what drives commodity price movements, and data scientists who want to extend the framework to new commodities or regions.

Whether you are a researcher looking for a new approach to an old problem, or an analyst trying to make sense of volatile markets — if you believe that good forecasts should come with good explanations, AGRICAF is for you.

The AGRICAF Framework

AGRICAF (Agricultural Commodity Forecasting Framework) is an open-source pipeline integrating multiple statistical and ML methods for forecasting global agricultural commodity prices (monthly, up to one year ahead).

The framework emphasises transparency and policy relevance, with a particular focus on making price forecasts and market intelligence accessible to non-specialist and vulnerable stakeholders in low-income contexts.

This Repository

This repository presents Stage 4 of AGRICAF: explainability and communication of forecasts across multiple audience levels.
